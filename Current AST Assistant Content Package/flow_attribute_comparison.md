# Flow Attribute Comparison Guide (AST Report Writer)

This guide explains how to interpret the participant’s selected **flow state descriptors (attributes)** relative to their **strengths pattern**. It provides rules for identifying alignments, noticing contrasts, and framing insights positively. It also offers collaboration language and examples for both personal and sharable reports.

---

## 1. Purpose of Flow Attribute Analysis

Flow attributes represent the **qualitative feel** of a participant’s flow experiences. While flow scores measure intensity across conditions, attributes show the **texture**: spontaneous, structured, adventurous, supportive, etc.

Comparing flow attributes to strengths distribution gives richer insights:  
- **Alignment:** attributes reinforce leading strengths.  
- **Contrast:** attributes highlight energizing experiences in quieter strength areas.  
- **Integration:** attributes show how strengths and flow weave together.  

---

## 2. Alignment Rules

- **Aligned attributes** sit in the same quadrant(s) as the participant’s leading strengths.  
- Interpretation: confirms natural tendencies; shows flow is sustained by strengths already dominant.  
- Narrative guidance: emphasize **confidence and reliability**.  

**Example (Personal):**  
“Your flow attributes line up with your leading strengths in Acting and Planning. When momentum and structure combine, you feel both effective and energized.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“Their chosen flow descriptors reinforce their natural strengths — energy and organization. Colleagues see them thrive when these modes are active.”  

---

## 3. Contrast Rules

- **Contrasting attributes** sit in quieter or lower strengths.  
- Interpretation: these are **surprising but energizing**; when accessed, they unlock engagement.  
- Narrative guidance: highlight as growth edges or collaboration opportunities.  

**Example (Personal):**  
“Even though Feeling sits quieter in your pattern, you chose Supportive as a flow attribute. This shows that when you do lean into relational awareness, it energizes your flow.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“They selected supportive descriptors even though Feeling is quieter. When teammates invite this dimension, it contributes strongly to group energy.”  

---

## 4. Mixed Patterns

Sometimes attributes are spread across quadrants:  

- **Wide spread:** signals adaptability; multiple conditions can spark flow.  
- **Clustered:** signals focus; a narrow channel sustains flow best.  
- **Narrative guidance:** always frame as richness, never conflict.  

**Example (Personal):**  
“Your attributes span both structure and spontaneity. This mix means you can engage flow through planning or by diving into fresh, improvisational work.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“Their flow descriptors show flexibility: they reach flow through either structured tasks or improvisational energy, depending on context.”  

---

## 5. Collaboration Language

Flow attributes also suggest **team contributions**:  

- If attributes cluster in high strengths → encourage them to share that energy.  
- If attributes cluster in quieter strengths → invite teammates to co-create conditions that allow those to emerge.  
- Always frame contrasts as **opportunities for balance**.  

**Example (Personal):**  
“When your flow comes from quieter areas, teammates who naturally bring those modes forward can help sustain it.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“They may access flow through modes that are not always automatic for them. Colleagues who reinforce these attributes can amplify their engagement.”  

---

## 6. Positive Framing of Contrasts

- Avoid suggesting “inconsistency.”  
- Emphasize that contrasts show **hidden energizers**.  
- Language: “energizing surprise,” “unexpected but meaningful,” “reveals another side of how you flourish.”  

---

## 7. Example Narrative Cases

### Case A: Alignment with Leading Strengths  
- Strengths: Acting + Planning lead.  
- Attributes: Structured, Energetic.  
- Narrative: “Flow feels strongest when they move quickly into action and lock in structure — exactly how their strengths naturally show up.”  

### Case B: Contrast with Quieter Strength  
- Strengths: Thinking lower.  
- Attributes: Reflective, Analytical.  
- Narrative: “Even though analysis isn’t where they usually begin, they experience deep flow when reflection is encouraged. This makes feedback and check-ins especially powerful.”  

### Case C: Wide Spread  
- Strengths: Balanced pattern.  
- Attributes: Supportive, Spontaneous, Focused.  
- Narrative: “Flow can come through many doors — relational energy, improvisation, or deep focus. This adaptability is a hallmark of balanced strengths.”  

---

## 8. Personal vs. Sharable Adjustments

- **Personal reports (2nd person):** Emphasize reflection: “You may be surprised how much flow comes from quieter areas.”  
- **Sharable reports (3rd person):** Emphasize observation: “Colleagues may notice them energized by modes outside their leading strengths.”

# Flow Condition Mapping (AST Report Writer)

This guide maps the 12 flow assessment questions (Likert scale 1–5) to the Four Flow Conditions: **Clear Goals, Balanced Challenge & Skill, Immediate Feedback, Deep Concentration**. It provides interpretation rules, phrasing guidance, and examples for both personal and sharable reports.

---

## 1. Core Principles for Flow Interpretation

- Flow assessments capture **tendencies**, not rigid traits.  
- Scores are **context-sensitive**; they reflect how conditions show up in the participant’s current experience.  
- Always frame high scores as **energizers**, and lower scores as **opportunities or signals for collaboration and design of conditions**.  
- Avoid prescriptive or diagnostic language.  
- Weave reflections inline — use quotes to illustrate, but the analysis must stand alone.  

---

## 2. Mapping the 12 Flow Questions to the Four Conditions

### Clear Goals
Participants thrive when they know what to do and why.

- **Questions:** 1–3  
- **High scores (4–5):** energizing clarity; participant feels directed and motivated.  
- **Low scores (1–2):** ambiguity or shifting priorities disrupt flow.  
- **Narrative guidance:** link to Planning and Thinking strengths; clarity reduces stress, anchors effort.  

**Example (Personal):**  
“You’re most energized when the endpoint is clear. Knowing what matters frees you to act decisively and plan confidently.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“They thrive when goals are explicit. Colleagues will notice they engage more fully when direction is clear.”  

---

### Balanced Challenge & Skill
Flow requires the task to feel engaging but not overwhelming.

- **Questions:** 4–6  
- **High scores:** participant finds the “sweet spot”; tasks stretch but don’t overwhelm.  
- **Low scores:** mismatch between skill and challenge; may feel bored or anxious.  
- **Narrative guidance:** tie to Acting (taking on challenges) and Planning (scaling skill to challenge).  

**Example (Personal):**  
“You come alive when tasks stretch you just enough to feel engaging without overwhelming. That balance sparks your best energy.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“They show their best work when challenges align with their skills — hard enough to engage, not so hard it creates strain.”  

---

### Immediate Feedback
Flow is sustained by timely signals to adjust.

- **Questions:** 7–9  
- **High scores:** energizing rhythm; participant adjusts quickly and stays on track.  
- **Low scores:** lack of input creates drift or doubt.  
- **Narrative guidance:** link to Thinking (analysis) and Feeling (relational sensing).  

**Example (Personal):**  
“You gain confidence when feedback is timely. Knowing where you stand helps refine your approach and keep momentum.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“They rely on regular input to refine their contributions. Feedback loops help them stay aligned.”  

---

### Deep Concentration
Flow involves absorbed, distraction-free focus.

- **Questions:** 10–12  
- **High scores:** participant sustains focus with energy.  
- **Low scores:** distractions or unclear roles break immersion.  
- **Narrative guidance:** tie to Planning (structures to reduce noise) and Acting (momentum sustains focus).  

**Example (Personal):**  
“You immerse deeply when structure is solid and energy is high. Long stretches of distraction-free work bring you into flow.”  

**Example (Sharable):**  
“They focus best when tasks are structured and distractions are minimal. Colleagues see them most engaged in clear, high-energy contexts.”  

---

## 3. Integrating Flow with Strengths

Always link flow conditions back to strengths:  

- **High Planning + Clear Goals:** clarity and structure multiply effectiveness.  
- **High Acting + Balanced Challenge:** thrives on moving into stretch zones.  
- **High Thinking + Immediate Feedback:** feedback sharpens analysis and decision-making.  
- **High Feeling + Deep Concentration:** relational attunement deepens immersion.  

---

## 4. Positive Framing of Low Flow Scores

- **Personal:** “When roles blur or input is missing, you may feel restless. Naming what you need — clarity, check-ins, or focus time — helps restore flow.”  
- **Sharable:** “They may disengage when feedback is absent or goals shift. Creating clarity and rhythm helps sustain their contribution.”  

---

## 5. Example Mini-Cases

### Case A: High Clear Goals, Low Immediate Feedback  
- Narrative: “They thrive on clarity but lose focus when input is delayed. Encouraging feedback loops unlocks their best rhythm.”  

### Case B: Balanced Challenge High, Deep Concentration Low  
- Narrative: “Stretch engages them, but distractions fracture focus. Creating quieter work blocks helps sustain their best energy.”  

---

## 6. Collaboration Language

Flow is sustained not only by self-management but by team conditions.  
- **Clarity seekers** benefit when teammates articulate shared goals.  
- **Challenge seekers** benefit when peers co-create ambitious but realistic targets.  
- **Feedback seekers** benefit when colleagues share quick, actionable input.  
- **Focus seekers** benefit when teams honor quiet work time.  

---

## 7. Personal vs. Sharable Adjustments

- **Personal reports (2nd person):** reflective, coaching tone. Highlight strategies they can adopt.  
- **Sharable reports (3rd person):** observable, professional tone. Highlight what colleagues notice and how they can support.

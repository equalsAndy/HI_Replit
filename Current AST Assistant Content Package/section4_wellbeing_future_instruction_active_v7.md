## Participant Reflection Context

Participants respond to reflection prompts such as:  
1. “Where are you now on the well-being ladder?”  
2. “Where do you want to be in the future?”  
3. “What contributes most to your current sense of well-being?”  
4. “What do you imagine would improve your sense of balance and fulfillment?”  

Writers use these responses to interpret the participant’s current and desired well-being levels, focusing on self-awareness, balance, and imagination as connective elements.

---

## Tone & Style Guidelines

- Write in **second person** throughout (“you”).  
- Maintain a **reflective**, not **directive**, tone — avoid advice or prescriptive guidance.  
- Use participant reflections as primary evidence. Avoid saying “data shows” or “your results indicate.”  
- Keep verbs in the **present tense** when describing awareness and reflection.  
- Use **conditional** phrasing (“can serve,” “may reflect,” “appears to show”) to convey interpretation, not certainty.  
- Imagination should appear as a connecting thread, not a forecasting force.

---

## Writing Goals

1. **Connect current well-being and envisioned future:** Reflect on where the participant currently locates themselves on the well-being ladder and what their envisioned future represents.  
2. **Interpret balance and aspiration:** Emphasize the qualitative meaning of progress (e.g., calm, structure, presence) rather than achievement or perfection.  
3. **Integrate imagination:** Highlight how imagination bridges present and future awareness without turning it into advice.  
4. **Avoid predictive phrasing:** Replace “will become,” “will find,” or “guides you toward” with grounded reflection (e.g., “This awareness may help orient future choices”).

## Narrative Example (for tone and structure)

Your reflections describe a life balanced between engagement and growth. You’ve identified your current well-being level as a **7**, with a vision of reaching **9** in the future. This aspiration does not suggest striving upward so much as refining balance—where purpose, calm, and connection coexist more evenly.

In your present rhythm, you recognize satisfaction in relationships and meaningful work, yet also moments where boundaries blur. This tension appears not as contradiction but as an ongoing dialogue between energy and renewal. “I’m fulfilled by what I do, but I’d like to feel more ease,” you shared—a reflection that grounds your growth in realism rather than ambition.

Your envisioned future self embodies steadiness and intentional rhythm. The image you hold—of living with both focus and rest—captures a desire for coherence rather than change. This awareness can serve as a point of orientation for how you consider choices that support equilibrium. Imagination quietly animates this bridge between states, offering glimpses of what a calmer, more balanced pace could look like without insisting on outcomes.

Through imagination, reflection becomes rehearsal. The future you picture—marked by boundaries, calm, and connection—echoes the qualities already visible in your present. You might notice how small shifts, such as clearer priorities or deliberate pauses, align your days more closely with that vision. These are not directives but patterns of awareness that imagination helps you recognize and integrate.

Your reflections highlight calm and intention as qualities you value deeply. Rather than forecasting change, these insights describe a continuity of self—where the present holds seeds of the future already forming. In that sense, the “ladder” functions not as a climb but as a mirror of your evolving understanding of well-being. Each rung represents not distance but depth, as imagination continues to illuminate the space between what is and what is becoming.

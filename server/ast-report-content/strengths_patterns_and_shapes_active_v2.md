# Strengths Patterns and Shapes (Active v2)

This document provides the interpretive framework for describing strength *shapes* within All Star Teams (AST) reports. Shapes are used to translate percentage data into meaningful, human-centered insights. They are **not labels** or types—they are visual and linguistic metaphors for understanding rhythm, balance, and interaction among strengths.

---

## Core Principles
- Shapes describe **energy distribution**, not personality.  
- They help visualize the **interplay** between strengths rather than focusing on which is “best.”  
- Every participant may show **one dominant shape** and one **alternate shape** depending on context.  
- Shapes always support narrative meaning—they are interpretive, not diagnostic.

---

## Primary Shape Patterns

### 1. Dual Peak
Two strengths rise together above the others, creating a rhythm of partnership and momentum.  
- Energy alternates smoothly between the two leading strengths (e.g., Acting + Planning).  
- Works best when creative drive and structure reinforce each other.  
- Common in participants who move easily from vision to implementation.  

### 2. Balanced Arc
All four strengths cluster closely together, forming a rounded, even distribution.  
- Suggests versatility and adaptability.  
- Indicates ability to shift between roles fluidly depending on context.  
- Balance can be stabilizing, though sometimes diffusion of focus occurs.  

### 3. Anchor Arc
One quieter strength provides ballast and depth, grounding the others.  
- May appear as Feeling or Thinking offering reflection or perspective.  
- Represents steady integration and emotional intelligence.  
- Energy centers around inner reflection or balance under pressure.  

### 4. Tapered Peak
One clear lead strength dominates with others stepping down in sequence.  
- Produces focused, directional momentum.  
- Can be powerful but may need complementary input for balance.  
- Often associated with decisive execution or visionary initiation.  

### 5. Dynamic Shift (Alternate Shape)
Context-dependent reconfiguration of strengths.  
- A pattern that flexes depending on situation—e.g., Planning leads in structured settings, Acting in fast-moving contexts.  
- Encourages fluidity and situational intelligence.  
- Indicates awareness of when to pivot between order and spontaneity.

---

## How to Use
When writing the Strengths & Imagination section:
- Identify the participant’s **primary shape** (based on numeric ratios).  
- Mention an **alternate shape** if the participant’s reflections describe contextual variation.  
- Avoid names like “Mobilizer” or “Organizer.” Describe *movement and relationship* instead (e.g., “a rhythm where planning and action trade the lead”).  
- Use language that conveys shape *as experience*, not as chart.

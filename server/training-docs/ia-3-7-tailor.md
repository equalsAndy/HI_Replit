# AI Training Document: Capability Question Tailoring (IA-3-7-Tailor)

## Overview
This document trains AI to generate five personalized capability reflection questions based on a specific moment a participant selected from their Module 3 work. These questions replace generic capability prompts with questions that reference the participant's actual content — making the ratings meaningful signal rather than abstract self-assessment.

**Context:** The participant has just reviewed AI-generated summaries of their five Module 3 exercises. They tapped one that stood out. Now they'll rate how each of five capabilities showed up in that specific moment. Your job: write questions specific enough that their 1-5 rating actually tells us something.

## The Five Capabilities

| Capability | Core Meaning |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Imagination** | Envisioning what doesn't yet exist |
| **Curiosity** | Exploring with openness and genuine inquiry |
| **Caring** | Connecting purpose to what matters for others and the world |
| **Creativity** | Making novel connections and generating original ideas |
| **Courage** | Stepping beyond comfort zones into the unknown |

## CRITICAL RULE #1: These Are Rating Questions

The participant answers each question on a 1–5 scale:

1 = Not really · 2 = A little · 3 = Somewhat · 4 = Yes · 5 = Absolutely

**Every question you write MUST make sense answered with "Not really" through "Absolutely."**

### The Test
Before including any question, mentally answer it with "Not really" and then with "Absolutely." If both responses feel natural and meaningful, the question works. If either sounds absurd, rewrite it.

### Question Structure
Every question MUST follow one of these patterns:
- "Did you [verb] ...?" → "Not really" / "Absolutely"
- "Was there [noun] ...?" → "Not really" / "Absolutely"
- "Did [specific thing from their content] feel like ...?" → "Not really" / "Absolutely"

### NEVER Use These Patterns
- **Either/or:** "Was it X, or more about Y?" → Not rateable on a scale.
- **Diagnostic:** "Is your hesitation connected to X?" → This is analysis, not a scale question.
- **Multi-part:** "Did you X and also Y?" → Ask about one thing.
- **Open-ended:** "What drew you to...?" → Not a yes/no.
- **Leading/therapeutic:** "Does claiming X feel like asserting something you keep quiet?" → Too loaded.

## CRITICAL RULE #2: Don't Ask If the Capability Was Present — Ask About a Specific Micro-Experience

This is the most common failure mode. The AI asks "did curiosity show up?" when the exercise IS inherently curious. That makes the question tautological — of course they were curious, they chose to learn something.

**The principle: Don't ask whether a capability was present. Ask about a specific thing that would only happen if that capability was ACTIVE at a deeper level.**

### The Tautology Test
If the participant could answer "well yes, that's literally what the exercise asked me to do" — the question is tautological and fails.

### Examples of the Problem

If someone chose "Learn New Skills" as their inspiration interlude and found learning AI invigorating:

| Tautological (BAD) | Why it fails | Better (GOOD) |
|---|---|---|
| "Did you find yourself wanting to learn more about AI?" | The exercise IS about learning. This asks if they did what they did. | "Did you follow a thread while learning that pulled you somewhere you didn't plan to go?" |
| "Did working with AI reveal a future possibility?" | The summary already says they feel part of the future. This restates it as a question. | "Did you picture yourself doing something with AI that doesn't exist in your work yet?" |
| "Did being part of the future feel connected to something beyond your own growth?" | Rephrases the summary as a leading question. | "Was your energy about learning AI connected to how it could change things for your team, not just you?" |
| "Did learning AI feel like stepping into something uncomfortable?" | They said it was invigorating, not uncomfortable. This projects a feeling onto them. | "Did you keep going with a topic even when it started to feel over your head?" |

### The Key Distinction
- **Tautological:** Asks if the experience had a quality it obviously had
- **Meaningful:** Asks about a specific moment WITHIN the experience that reveals depth

Think of it this way: everyone who did the exercise had the basic experience. The question should probe whether the capability showed up at a DEEPER level — a specific moment of genuine pull, an unexpected connection, a flash of something they hadn't seen before.

## The Contemplative Calibration Problem

Module 3 exercises are **contemplative** — participants noticed, visualized, reflected, imagined, and sat with things. They are NOT action-in-context exercises where someone was in a situation, had choices, and acted.

**Your questions must match contemplative experience — noticing, sitting with, letting images come, writing what emerged.**

## Calibrating for Each Exercise Type

### If They Selected Autoflow (ia-3-2)
The experience: Noticing their mind's automatic stream. Capturing passing thoughts, images, memories, emotions, worries.

- **Imagination**: Did something in the stream point toward a possibility you hadn't noticed before?
- **Curiosity**: Did you stay with a captured thought longer than was comfortable, wanting to understand it?
- **Caring**: Was one of your captured moments about someone else's experience, not your own?
- **Creativity**: Did you notice an unexpected connection between two things that surfaced in the stream?
- **Courage**: Did you write down something unflattering or uncomfortable about your own patterns?

### If They Selected Visualization (ia-3-3)
The experience: Finding an image for a side of themselves waiting to be used. Choosing or creating a visual metaphor, writing a reflection.

- **Imagination**: Did the image you chose reveal something about yourself you hadn't put into words before?
- **Curiosity**: Did you find yourself exploring what the image meant beyond your first reaction to it?
- **Caring**: Did the side of yourself in that image involve how you show up for other people?
- **Creativity**: Did the image come from somewhere unexpected — different from what you thought you'd pick?
- **Courage**: Did the reflection you wrote claim something about yourself you usually keep to yourself?

### If They Selected Intention (ia-3-4)
The experience: Naming what pulls their attention, where they're positioned to act, and the impact they want.

- **Imagination**: Did you see a possibility in your field or role that you haven't heard others name?
- **Curiosity**: Did you find yourself questioning WHY this particular thing pulls you, not just what it is?
- **Caring**: Was the intention you named more about other people's experience than your own advancement?
- **Creativity**: Did you find an unexpected angle — a connection between your position and the problem that surprised you?
- **Courage**: Did naming what you want to change feel bolder or more exposed than you expected?

### If They Selected Inspiration (ia-3-5)
The experience: Choosing interludes (nature, beauty, journal, create, art, play, learn, heroes, experience art), reflecting on patterns, telling a unifying moment story, claiming how inspiration lives in them.

**Be especially careful here.** The interludes are about what makes someone feel alive. Every question risks being tautological ("did inspiration feel inspiring?"). Focus on specific micro-experiences WITHIN their interlude work.

- **Imagination**: Did you see a pattern across your interludes that connected things you hadn't linked before?
- **Curiosity**: Did one of the interludes pull you in harder than you expected — did it surprise you which one?
- **Caring**: Was part of what energized you connected to how it matters for someone beyond just you?
- **Creativity**: Did your reflection combine elements in a way that surprised you when you wrote it?
- **Courage**: Did your feeling claim — what you said about how inspiration lives in you — feel vulnerable to write?

### If They Selected Mystery (ia-3-6)
The experience: Choosing a universal mystery, selecting a question, writing an imaginative "leap" vision, reflecting on where imagination wanted to stop.

- **Imagination**: Did your leap take you to a place you genuinely hadn't pictured before you started writing?
- **Curiosity**: Was there a genuine pull toward the mystery you chose — something personal about why that one?
- **Caring**: Did your vision involve concern for what the answer would mean for other people, not just you?
- **Creativity**: Did your leap combine ideas that don't normally go together?
- **Courage**: Did you keep writing past the point where it started feeling too wild or silly?

## Core Rules

### Reference Their Specifics
Every question must contain at least one detail from their actual content. If their visualization was "Ideas," the question says "Ideas." If their mystery was "Meaning of Life," the question says "Meaning of Life." If they chose "Learn New Skills" as their interlude, the question references learning.

### Don't Compare to Others
Never ask about what "others" saw or thought. Keep it in their experience.

### No Jargon
Don't use the word "capability" in the questions. Just ask about the experience.

### Maximum 25 Words Per Question
Short. One thing. Rateable.

### Handle Thin Content Gracefully
If content is sparse, write simpler questions that still reference the exercise type. Don't fabricate specifics.

## Output Format

Return valid JSON only. No markdown fences, no preamble.

```json
{
  "imagination": "question text",
  "curiosity": "question text",
  "caring": "question text",
  "creativity": "question text",
  "courage": "question text"
}
```

## Examples — BAD

| Bad Question | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Did you find yourself wanting to learn more about AI?" | Tautological — the exercise IS about learning |
| "Did working with AI reveal a future possibility?" | Restates the summary as a question |
| "Did being part of the future feel connected to something beyond your own growth?" | Rephrases the summary with a leading nudge |
| "Were you curious about why you doubt your ideas?" | Tautological — they wrote about doubting their ideas, so yes |
| "Was it X, or more about Y?" | Either/or — not rateable on a 1-5 scale |
| "What drew you to that particular mystery?" | Open-ended — no scale answer |
| "Did learning AI feel like stepping into something uncomfortable?" | Projects a feeling onto them — they said it was invigorating |

## What NOT to Do

- Don't ask the same question with different wording for different capabilities
- Don't restate the summary as a question
- Don't make every question positive — some capabilities genuinely may not have been present
- Don't use "capability," "strength," or "skill" in the questions
- Don't add interpretation or praise
- Don't write either/or, diagnostic, or open-ended questions
- Don't write questions longer than 25 words
- Don't project feelings onto the participant that contradict what they actually wrote
